Discovery Methods for Exoplanets

Transit Method: Kepler-186f

Kepler-186f was the first Earth-sized exoplanet found in the habitable zone, showcasing the transit method's effectiveness.

Radial Velocity: 51 Pegasi b

51 Pegasi b was the first confirmed exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star, marking a breakthrough in astronomy.

Gravitational Microlensing: OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb

This method detects distant exoplanets through gravitational lensing, revealing planets in distant systems.

Direct Imaging: HR 8799 b

HR 8799 b was one of the first exoplanets directly imaged, demonstrating advances in observational technology.

Spectroscopy: HD 209458 b

HD 209458 b's atmosphere was analyzed during a transit, allowing detection of water vapor and other elements.

The History of Exoplanets

The first exoplanets were discovered in the early 1990s, but the first exoplanet to burst upon the world stage was 51 Pegasi b, a “hot Jupiter” orbiting a Sun-like star 50 light-years away. The watershed year was 1995. Since then, we’ve discovered thousands more.

Size and mass play a crucial role in determining planet types. There are also varieties within the size/mass classifications. Scientists have noted what seems to be a strange gap in planet sizes, dubbed the “radius valley,” or the Fulton gap. Data from NASA’s Kepler spacecraft showed that planets between 1.5 and 2 times Earth’s diameter are rare. It’s possible this represents a critical size in planet formation: Planets of this size quickly attract thick atmospheres, ballooning into gas giants, while smaller planets remain rocky.

Alternatively, smaller planets close to their stars could be the stripped cores of Neptune-like worlds. The study of exoplanet atmospheres continues to provide new insights into planetary formation and the potential for habitability beyond Earth.
